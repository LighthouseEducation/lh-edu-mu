Our Learning Campus

Our Learning Campus in Calebasses, Mauritius How our site and buildings reflect who we are and promote learning

In 2017, Lighthouse inaugurated its learning campus and thanked God for its provision. Though the secondary school had been holding classes here since 2016, it was the first year that the entire school, pre-primary through secondary learners, joined together.

The school acquired the 11 arpents of land in December 2013 and immediately took to planning an innovative campus that would be built out in stages in step with the school’s growth and, of course, availability of financing (a mix of loan and donations). This particular location was chosen because:

  • It is central to the northern region and close to important road links such as the highway intersecting the island, the ring road providing quick access to residential areas on the other side of Port Louis and two other bypass roads.
  • It is uniquely situated adjacent to a forest and a river.
  • It forms part of a diverse residential community, providing easy links to community and giving our students and families an opportunity to make a difference in our immediate area.

(For some of the ideas in the remainder of this article, we would like to thank Green School of Bali, Indonesia for providing inspiration in its “Three Springs” concept of school architecture and landscaping.)

Lighthouse aims to be a fulfilling, self-regulating organisation and its campus a place of renewal that will inherently have meaning and purpose, providing children and young people with a stimulating, entrepreneurial, and sustainable environment in which to develop and learn by example.

Essentially the learning neighbourhood is conceived of as a village, containing three interlocking functions: educational, social, and promotion of working lives.

The educational centre

The nucleus will be a holistic, student-centred school. The school will provide a genuine heart to the whole enterprise, where physical (KQ), emotional (EQ), intellectual (IQ), and inspirational (SQ) capacities are prized. Homes, farms, businesses, and individuals attached to the neighbourhood, in the enterprise and social centres, will have a direct, practical link to the educational provision. The school ultimately does not stand apart from the surrounding community of Calebasses but is a valued member of it.

The school’s buildings and beautiful grounds will be a learning place and a meeting place where students, families and guests feel welcome.

School Design Concepts

  • The Hub serves as the centre for reception, administration, idea exchange and as an exhibition area for student work and visiting artists. Its concept is to be light, airy, welcoming and simple, with a double-volume space to promote cooling ventilation and light as well as representing an upward focus.
  • The Pavilion is an open-air meeting space where children eat together in a family atmosphere, celebrations happen and physical activities can take place in any weather year-round. The Pavilion is near the Boulevard, a natural tree-lined walking path which bisects the campus and, besides acting as a service path for deliveries to the kitchen, can host farmers’ market tables and special activities.
  • Classrooms are properly oriented to account for maximum air ventilation, use of natural light and two full walls per class of windows. Plants and creeping vines soften the exterior as well as promote cooling. External ‘art panels’ will house artist-directed and student-executed art. All classrooms have doors directly to the exterior and walking paths encourage regular outdoor exposure throughout the day. Classroom size is at an optimal standard to allow for active, experiential learning and classrooms include home-like elements. Classrooms are orderly – everything in its place – yet creative and ever-changing, in step with learning. Students are at the centre, not a teacher’s desk.
  • A Library features prominently for all-campus use yet promotes a quiet, soothing atmosphere. It looks on to the forest and its interior design allows for independent reading and discovery, small group work supervised through glass partitioning and technology use. A small student theatre is integrated into the design.
  • Innovative laboratories as well as design and technology workshops are placed for maximum integration into learning and student life. Students use the workshops to build campus features themselves.
  • Organic gardens and play fields surround the classrooms. Gardens are well-defined and under student responsibility. Gardens also supply the school kitchen with fresh ingredients. Play equipment is made up mostly of natural or recycled materials and is non-prescriptive – allowing the imagination to run wild.
  • Integrated into the landscape are learning hamlets – outdoor learning spaces to encourage creativity and imagination, defined by natural objects.
  • Children can be exposed to animal habitats such as an apiary, mobile chicken coops and turtle ponds, as well as the natural fauna of the forest.
  • The landscape is multi-leveled and variable, providing nooks and crannies – quiet spaces and features for the eye, ear, and heart to play with, as well as rocks to climb, a natural amphitheatre and water reticulation features.
  • There are outdoor shade structures as well as features for encouraging sunny areas and the play of dappled light. Trees are identifiable in common and scientific names and cared for by the students.
  • All services within the buildings will be sustainable and sensitive to the wider environment, e.g. water heating through solar power; rainwater is collected in tanks for use in irrigation; and eventually the school will generate its own electricity. Food and garden waste is composted and managed by the students. The majority of spaces on the campus are designed to operate without artificial air-conditioning even though the school operates in a tropical climate, thus promoting environmental sustainability and optimal health. Likewise, natural light is used the majority of daylight time. A fibre optic network is accessible campus-wide and in outdoor areas, yet monitored and firewalled to protect children.
  • The forest and riverbanks provide daily inspiration to learners and are protected by them. In curricular activities as well as special clubs (such as “Forest School”) learners are encouraged to be in and engage with nature. Near the forest, students study environmental management as well as agriculture in dedicated gardening beds, endemic plant nurseries and living science labs.

Wider Community Impact

Under the umbrella of The Lighthouse Trust, Lighthouse Primary and Secondary School would like to the development of other community-based activities growing out from its campus. These might include:

  • Promotion of kitchen gardens and composting in surrounding homes;
  • Other income-generating businesses that help train unskilled workers, provide apprenticeships for our students and provide some funding for the Lighthouse scholarship fund;
  • A community centre and meeting space, possibly for a market for surrounding smallholder farmers; and Development of community nature trails which are managed, cleaned and protected.

Children who attend Lighthouse can expect to be a part of a dynamic, changing campus for the years to come. Some of the concepts presented here are in place and others in development. It is expected that families who are a part of this community take this into due consideration and are together with us as the dream develops.

Why school lunches matter

Since its inception, Lighthouse has always provided meals to all of its students. When there is so much work to be done in delivering reading, writing and arithmetic (as well as many other academic subjects!), why did we take on the extra work of basically running a child-focused restaurant that feeds about 300 people per day?

Because food is really, really important! What children (and adults) take into their growing bodies has an enormous impact on their ability to learn and process.

An enthusiastic team of people help plan and prepare a menu that supports Lighthouse’s mission. In addition to lunch, children receive a mid-morning snack of fresh fruit every day and frequently other healthy treats. We also aim to expose children to teaching on nutrition and give them opportunities to cultivate their own food on campus.

Secondary students also take ownership by helping in food service on a roster basis as well as working on mealtime improvement through student-led clubs.

Why do we require that all students eat the snacks and lunch provided by the school?

  • To promote high nutritional standards: students benefit from a varied diet with a high component of vegetables and fresh/raw ingredients. It is a challenge to provide variety and freshness in an individual packed lunch from home, particularly in a tropical climate.
  • To ensure optimal readiness for learning: research proves that nutrition impacts brain function.
  • To reduce waste: students may eat as much as they wish but are monitored to discourage waste. By preparing lunch at school and serving it fresh, we also avoid a lot of packaging waste.
  • To minimize social and cultural barriers: food and mealtimes can either enforce or break down group-based identity. We want our students to unite as a school community, appreciating a wide range of food which is served to everyone equally. At the same time, we are responsive to food restrictions based on religious grounds or allergies/other medical conditions.
  • To involve students in the production of their own food: by identifying food origins and being involved in food preparation, students become better eaters and conscientious consumers.
  • To cultivate healthy eating habits and culture: research has found that eating together as a family (sharing the same food) helps children learn how to make healthy food choices, to communicate with others and to master table manners. Lighthouse promotes a strong ‘family’ culture within our school we can build to the development of healthy eating habits for our students. Food brings people together. Our secondary students even came up with table arrangements to ensure no one feels left out at meal time.

We understand that young children in particular can have a limited palate and show ‘pickiness.’ Again, research has shown that patient and repeated exposure to different food helps children out of this. Our meal programme aims to gently help them develop their taste and appreciation for a wider variety of food, for their own health.
